Part 1: Explanation Through Narrative — How Does a Baby Learn About "Triangle"?

To understand the core of this theory, let's follow the story of how a baby who cannot yet speak comes to recognize a single "triangular building block."

Step 1: First Contact (Noise)

The baby gropes and touches the "edge" of the building block. As the finger slides, only the sensation of "straightness" continues. This is merely a flow of information (noise) that has no meaning yet.

Step 2: Encountering a Corner (Change)

When the finger reaches a "corner," the direction suddenly changes. This is also just a one-time "change" at this point.

Step 3: Tracing the Outline (Pattern Discovery)

The baby traces the outline of the building block all the way around. Then, there are three instances of the change "turning at a corner," and the baby returns to the starting point.

Step 4: The "Aha!" Moment (2D "Closure")

The baby traces the outline once more. Then, the baby notices that the finger turns in exactly the same order and at the same angles as before. At this moment, a revolution occurs in the baby's mind: "This isn't random information. This is something with a repeating, fixed pattern!"

This is the most important concept in RAIN theory: "Closure". When the meaningless flow of information satisfies two conditions—"the trajectory is closed (returns to the same place)" and "the pattern repeats (is periodic)"—the seed of meaning is born for the first time.

Step 5: Naming the Meaning (Rhythm ID)

This repeating unique pattern of "turning at specific angles three times and returning" is the "Rhythm ID" that captures the essence of this shape. The baby doesn't yet know the word "triangle." For the baby, the true identity of "triangle" is this tactile rhythm itself.

Step 6: Sensory Integration (Resonance)

Next, suppose someone taps the building block and lets the baby hear the wooden sound. This "tap-tap" sound also has its own unique rhythm. The baby learns that "whenever I feel that 'tactile rhythm' (Rhythm ID), I always hear this 'sound rhythm.'"

Thus, the tactile Rhythm ID and the auditory Rhythm ID begin to "resonate" by always appearing together in space-time. This makes the baby's recognition of "this building block" richer and more certain.

Step 7: Understanding Three-Dimensional Forms (Higher-Dimensional "Closure")

Now, a 2D outline is a single path, so it's easy to determine if it's "closed" by tracing it once and checking if the rhythm repeats. However, a 3D solid is not a single path. Understanding this requires the concept of "closure" to become higher-dimensional.

The baby picks up the building block, flips it over, and observes it from various angles:

  • Looking from the front, finding one Rhythm ID
  • Tilting it slightly, finding another new Rhythm ID
  • Looking from the side, finding yet another different Rhythm ID

After playing with it sufficiently, a saturation state arrives where "no new Rhythm ID can be found from any angle." This moment when "new discoveries are exhausted" is the "closed" state in three-dimensional space. This integrates the scattered recognition of faces and completes the unity as a solid.
